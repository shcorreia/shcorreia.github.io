Short Stories by S. Correia

Nothing Left but Seeing

Nothing Left but Seeing We had gotten married one year ago today. I dreamt of her last week. I dream of her most nights. We were at the oceanside, her face shining in the sun. The next night, I dreamt of her in the English field, traveling across an ocean to see me.

I had been in Ireland for a week. She never got to go. We had talked about going after we got married, but then we got married at home and she was too sick to leave. And it became a “someday” until it wasn’t. Until there wasn’t any time left.

After she died, I left my job. I had an office job at a place near our apartment so that I could be with her more. I didn’t really like the people I worked with, but it paid pretty well and I was always able to take her to appointments.

I had started out in London, then in Oxford. I cried for six days straight. I took all of the pain and memories of us being here together and let them kill me from the inside out. I screamed at the Thames and people avoided me. I must have looked ragged and mean.

I then went to Manchester and Cambridge. Then Wales for a bit. Nobody knew me there. It was what I wanted.

And now I’m in Ireland. My flight back to Boston is in a week. I’ve been away for a long time, time that I thought I would spend healing. And I know things are different now. I don’t know if I can ever heal. But I probably don’t want to.

I had only brought three paperback books with me. To pack light. I read them each twice, and spent a lot of time in random local libraries, reading.

She got sick when I was finishing up school. We didn’t really have money, not really. It was hard, so hard, for such a long time.

A woman in a pub asked me if I was married. I was still wearing my ring. I looked down at my ring. “Yeah, I guess I am.” She had a look on her face that made me think that she knew I was lying. But she didn’t say anything.

I tried not to drink too much, because it just made it worse. Replacing coffee with wine, even at night. Staying up and sleeping in.

I had been in Galway for a few days and was staying on a houseboat. Living on bread and alcohol and barely anything. I’d become thin with worry and exhaustion and constant movement. Thinner than I had been when I was running and when she was sick.

I told people that I was okay, but I was doing much worse than that. They told me I should stick around family, that I shouldn’t be by myself. But I couldn’t stand being around anyone that I knew. The monotonous choices of the life that I knew before seemed so dull and pointless.

I’d have to go back to our apartment. To the plants, pictures of us, our clothes, the paintings. And find a new job, do something. Try not to drink all the time. Try to spend time with people. People that knew her. People that knew us, together. People that were there, at our wedding. We got married because we knew that we didn’t have time. We promised each other forever, trying to grasp at any time left.

I felt like I didn’t have anything else left. Seeing these places without her, because it’s what I promised her. Of all of the things that I promised her, I think that this is the only thing that I could do right.

The Ghost in Your Home

It wasn’t until after I died that I realized that ghost stories aren’t really about the ghosts at all. They’re about the person who is still alive, the one left behind. The one who sees the ghost and is afraid to be in their own home.

I never got to tell him. Ben. I saw him every day for three years. We were friends. And I never told him.

I woke up in bed feeling strange. Like I was still half asleep, that feeling of not being fully awake. But not in the way of like, being on the subway at 7am with your coffee in your hand and angry at the world. Just content. Like I was aware of myself, but could easily go back to sleep if I wanted to. I left my room and padded down the stairs. I started to make coffee, but it took a while. I would forget the filter, then forget the water. I figured I must still be tired. My body felt kind of sore, like I had gone to the gym the previous day. I hadn’t, but maybe I just slept weird last night.

Nobody was home, but that wasn’t strange. My roommates were probably all working today. I should probably know everyone’s schedule by now. I was waiting for my coffee to finish and went to go flip through the newspaper that was on the kitchen table. I’m glad that Tilly insists on buying the newspaper still, to support journalism and all that, but I usually forget to read it. And that’s when it. My face staring back at me. Under the obituaries section.

I stared at it, for a while, confused. I was still really tired. I figured it must be a mistake, just some girl who looks a lot like me. Some other girl with my exact same name and age and hometown. But that’s when it came flooding back. The car accident on the way back from the show Tuesday night, an oncoming car skidding in the snow on the highway. There was impact, and pain. And then I couldn’t remember. How long ago was that? I must have gotten home somehow, right?

Every possible explanation rushed through my head. Maybe I had been in the hospital. In a coma. Or I had slight amnesia. Or my brain was just forgetting the traumatic event of being hit by an oncoming car.

I looked at it again. My obituary. I felt an immediate panic. They must have it wrong. I’m alive. I’m here. But as soon as I thought it, I knew it wasn’t true. I just knew somehow. I thought my breath had been speeding up, on the verge of a panic attack. But then I realized that my chest was moving only out of muscle memory. Air wasn’t getting into my lungs at all.

If this is just some dream that I’m having, like a bad sci-fi movie, and I’ll wake up and everything will be fine in the morning, then I really need to stop drinking coffee before bed.

The coffee machine beeped to indicate that the coffee was done, but I didn’t move. I read the obituary. I read it five times. “Her funeral will be held on Friday, January 22nd, at 10am. I looked at the time.” The clock said 10:37. So maybe that’s where my roommates were. At my funeral. Jesus Christ.

If I’m really dead, and if there really is a funeral, then that’s where they better be. And if they’re skipping out on my funeral, I am so going to hound them for it for the rest of their lives.

I didn’t know what to do, or why I was in my kitchen, if I had died. Maybe it was some sort of mistake. I suppose I could wait around for my roommates to get back, but that could be ages. I couldn’t just stay in the house by myself. My body felt all itchy. I had to do something, anything. The idea of sitting on the couch and watching television seemed ridiculous, and almost made me laugh. Some Buffy perhaps? Or Dark Shadows?

I left the house and just started walking. I walked for 20 minutes to the subway, and people kept bumping in to me. A few times I said “sorry” but they just looked at me, confused.

Once I got on the subway, things seemed different. People weren’t bumping in to me anymore; it was almost like my body could pass through theirs. Maybe I was fading as time went on, but it hadn’t even been an hour since I had left the house. Clearly, I was running out of time. I suppose they couldn’t see me and thought they could stand where I was standing. I tried to calm my breathing. Or whatever fake-breathing that I was doing, because clearly breathing didn’t matter anymore. Things would be fine. Everything would be fine.

I got off at the subway stop near his apartment. I didn’t realize where I was going until I stepped off the train. I didn’t have a plan. What was I going to do? Every ghost story that I ever read was frightening. Would I even be able to talk to him? Maybe he won’t even be home.

I checked my watch. It was 11:42. The sun was bright in the sky, a perfect shade of blue. I could text him. No, I couldn’t. If the funeral thing was real, if I were really dead—and I was starting to believe it—there was no way I could text him. He would think it was some sick joke.

I had been to his apartment only two or three times. Every time it had been late, after the bar, when we were sort of drunk. We would go to watch our favorite baking show and drunkenly eat ice cream from his fridge, before I would walk back to the subway. Each time I had to resist the urge to kiss him. I had always thought it would be a terrible idea, at the time.

I stood outside his apartment for some time. I had no idea what to do. But I knew I had to talk to him, somehow, and tell him everything. It was too late; maybe I was making it worse. I wanted to cry, but instead I just felt empty, like I was slipping away.

But something in me made me try the door. He was way too trusting and often left his apartment unlocked if he was home, so I just walked in.

I didn’t see him. Maybe he wasn’t home and had forgotten to lock his door. But that’s when I heard him moving around in his bedroom. I figured I would just wait for him to leave his room. But talking to him would probably freak him out. Should I write him a note? I saw his notebook on the kitchen counter, filled with his sketches.

I had asked him to draw me just a week ago. We were sitting in the bar, two pints in. It took him five minutes, and it was easily the best drawing I had ever seen. I had asked if I could keep it. And that’s when I remembered that a few nights ago, during the crash, I had been wearing the jeans that I had worn from that night last week. To my dismay, I reached into my back pocket and pulled out the drawing. He would know that I was the one who had it.

I grabbed the pen that was near his notebook and wrote “Ben, it’ll be okay. Don’t freak out, please just listen” below the drawing. I would talk to him. I could do it.

I started to panic again. What if he didn’t know? No no, he would know, he probably found out when I didn’t show up to work for the last few days. Or worse, what if he didn’t care? I mean, we were friends, but what if he really didn’t care? Or had already gotten over it already?

I waited another few minutes, but he hadn’t left his room. I know it was bad, but I opened his sketchbook. I know how private those things are; I would kill anyone who read my journals. But I just needed to see more of his work; it felt like the only thing that could calm me down.

I flipped through pages of meeting notes from work. But what I noticed was a lot of drawings of me. In the staff meeting margins, next to math calculations. I was shocked. I had never seen these before. And I would look at him whenever I could, when he was drawing and I thought he wouldn’t notice. Because he was beautiful. But he must have drawn me a dozen times throughout the notebook. I was so surprised that I dropped the notebook on the ground.

That’s when John gingerly left his room, presumably confused by the noise. He thought he was alone. He stared at the notebook on the floor; I could practically see himself trying to rationalize it in his mind. Maybe it had slid off the table. Clearly, he couldn’t see me.

As he went to pick up the notebook, that’s when he saw the note. My face, staring back at him. Creases in the page from being in my pocket, the ink from my pen still fresh. He looked visibly shaken. He gripped the edge of the counter, shaking his head. “What the hell, this can’t be happening” he said in a whisper to himself.

“Ben.” My voice, thin and shaky, filled the silence in the kitchen. I sounded like I was underwater. He whipped his head around. Confusion and anger were still scattered on his face. I focused really hard on wanting him to see me. And his face changed from anger to shock. I knew he could see me, at least a little bit. It took a lot of focus, but I could stay half-visible. He continued to stare in disbelief.

“Ben, please just listen. It’s me. I don’t know what happened.”

“Alice. Alice. What’s going on. This isn’t real.”

“It is real, I am real, at least for a little bit. It’s me.”

“You died. They buried you this morning. I was there.” “Ah, yeah, surprise.” And I don’t know why, but I started laughing. It was all so ridiculous.

Unsurprisingly, he didn’t share my humor.

“What’s going on?” He said.

I shook my head. “I don’t know. But I had to find you.” I kept going, couldn’t stop myself from rambling on. “You have to know by now. That I love you.” I paused to catch my breath. “I’ve loved you for years. I should have told you earlier, before…all of this. I never knew what to say. But I love you. Really, truly, terribly. And I hate that I never told you. I’m sorry.”

My heart was breaking. I just wanted him to say something. Even if he didn’t love me back. When he didn’t say anything, I just said “I know you don’t love me back, not that way, but I just had to tell you, so I didn’t regret it for the rest of my existence. However long that is” I said with a slight laugh that didn’t have any real humor to it.

He shook his head and smiled. He actually smiled. “How could you think that I don’t love you?”

I shook my head. I was shocked. “I…”

He kept going, looking me in the eye this time. “I’ve barely left my room since I heard the news. I wish I had all the time left in the world with you, because there’s nothing that I would rather do then spend my time with you. And now I have a second chance, for whatever reason, for however long.” He said, looking at my fading hands that were becoming more transparent by the second. “And I’m not going to fuck it up this time. I’ve wanted to tell you every day for a long, long time. I just didn’t have the words. Words were always more your thing anyways.” He had a half-smirk, half-smile on his face. “But of course I love you. And the fact that I won’t get to see you anymore is devastating. But at least you came back. At least I got to tell you.”

He was smiling and crying a bit, but then he noticed that I was almost gone. I guess my form had lasted long enough to do the one thing that I should have done in life.

“Don’t leave me again, please” he said, trying to grab my hand, but it wouldn’t work.

“I’m sorry if I made this worse” I admitted, worried suddenly that maybe I shouldn’t have come back here at all. This was probably only making things worse for him.

“Never apologize” he said, attempting to cup my face but really only cupping the space where my face had been only a moment ago. Time was running out.

“I love you” I said, determined to say it one more time.

“I love you too. Please don’t forget it.”

“I won’t,” I promised him. And while the last of my body disappeared, I knew that no matter what happened, I truly wouldn’t forget, no matter what.

30 years

My father has been in prison for half of my life. He had killed his father for the things that he did to my father’s sister. When he found out what had been happening to his sister, he killed his father out of anger and vengeance. He knew it would be the only way to stop him. For a long time, I resented the fact that my father was in prison, but I understand why he killed his father.

I visit him as often as I can. We sit across the table and talk. We write letters to each other too, even though it’s outdated. I keep every letter in a box under my bed. I read them and reread them.

I send him books. I go to the local bookstore and have them send the books to the prison. The booksellers are always a bit confused, but they’ve gotten used to me now. He can never keep the books, but I don’t care. He says the prison library is shit.

Last month, I went to the book store and sent him Dracula. I always read the books with him. In his letters, he talked about the representations of modernity in the novel. He was an English teacher, before he was sent to prison. But he doesn’t try to teach me the books. He’s always just interested in seeing my thoughts.

A few months back we read Andy Weir’s The Martian. My father told me that he identified with a person being stuck and alone. He said he was ready to go home, to go back to Earth. I cried when I read that in his letter, because for the most part he didn’t talk about his feelings much. He didn’t talk about prison, other than jokes about the food and making friends. But I knew that it was killing him.

Two years ago, I sent him The Hobbit. It was my favorite book as a kid. He read it to me every night for two years. I could practically recite it when I turned three years old. I still had my copy, after all those years. The pages were practically falling out, but I read it while he read it behind bars.

My father doesn’t regret what he did. Or so he tells me. But he tells me that he regrets missing so much of my life. He also says he misses his students, the kids who have so much life ahead of them.

He also sends me some stories that he’s been working on. I have pages and pages of his stories. I type them up and keep them. I don’t know why I type them. I guess I just feel like it’s important to have his work stored somewhere, instead of just on the pages that he writes by hand.

I’m hoping he’ll get out for good behavior. I was shocked by the verdict. I really thought that he wouldn’t be convicted, because of the things that my grandfather did. I cried for weeks. It was the hardest thing in the world to say goodbye. He still has at least 17 years left in his sentence, if he gets the full term. I’ll be 43. And I think about what that moment will be like every single day.

The Information Collector

I have read your journal. Even though you don’t know me. I’ve been in your room and know where you hide things. I know that you carry around a journal in your bag that you write in. But I know that, when you’re really angry and scared, you write in the journal that’s on your bookshelf, between the book on music theory and your copy of The Things They’ve Carried that you’ve never read. The journal that you carry in your bag is one that your mother gave you; it is blue.

In your journal—the secret one—you write about how you think about dying a lot and what it would mean for your family. You write about getting rid of your knives. I can’t tell if you’ve actually gotten rid of them yet, but I cannot find them anywhere in your room. I think that is a good thing. I am very thorough, and would have found them.

I collect secrets—more specifically, information. I don’t do anything with it. I don’t post it online and I certainly don’t tell anyone. But I feel that it’s my job to know. To know you. No one else knows you like I know you. Not your doctor or your mother, and certainly not your spouse. Isn’t that all anyone wants? To connect? To be seen and understood? I understand you. I love you.

And you there, I have read your files. The ones that you keep in a locked file cabinet in your office. Well, it was locked. Then unlocked. But I locked it back up when I left, for safety. I read your will, and how you’re giving everything to the friend that you’ve been in love with for forty years, and nothing to the nephew that you despise. Your health files, showing medical procedures and chemo treatments. Your bills piling up.

Everyone has secrets. Those who don’t think that they have secrets usually have the best ones. And I know them all.

Your letter to your boyfriend was well written—detailed, but not overly sentimental. I know you’re uncertain about breaking up with him, as is evident from the fact that the letter has been sitting in your desk drawer for two weeks. I think you should give it to him. I think he’ll appreciate the letter. Though, to be fair, he probably won’t appreciate being broken up with.

The man standing near the doors on the train. His laptop should really have a better password. He donates monthly to an organization for gay teens. He is a writer, but doesn’t let anyone read his work. I’ve checked his email, and he’s never sent it to anyone. He lies all the time. He’s probably gay, but it doesn’t appear that anyone knows about it. Other than me. Don’t worry. His secret is safe with me.

You might wonder how I remember everything. I don’t. I keep everything. Pictures, copies, all of it. I write it down, sort through it. And say, even if I were to take things from your home, would you notice? And if you did notice, you wouldn’t know that it was me. You don’t even know me.

Minutes on the Watch

I need more want more my god the seconds move by and I am wasting them. I’m wasting away and every day that I’m not doing something astounding I can feel my cells dying and the blood moving in my body. I am losing track of what’s important in life god I thought I knew. I am so lonely it feels like suffocating from space. The days blend together, I feel like I’m not doing anything my god what have I done.

I think what I’ve learned is that everyone is extremely lonely and we’re all just trying to connect with one another in the horribly brief time that we have.

I talk to myself. Perhaps I’m going mad. I smashed my watch. I threw it across the room and then stepped on it, cracking the glass. The ticking still continued in my brain, even after.

I still don’t know how to do this or how to cry or laugh. My hands are shaking and I can’t sit still. My breath rattles and I know my breaths are limited. How many breaths do we have left together?

Boxes

There was a person who got lost once. She was about to move out, which is a thing that people do all of the time. She told herself this as she worried about moving. People move out every day, probably. But do people move out of their childhood bedrooms every day? Probably not. Do people worry about upsetting their parents as much as she does? Maybe. And so, she packed. It was a task that was easy for her but also the hardest thing in the world. Twenty-two years’ worth of stuff, shoved into boxes. A life left to a box. And to make it worse, she didn’t know how to talk to her parents.

She had been in the house for too long. She decided to go on a walk even though the air was thick and humid and oppressive. She walked for a while and tried to admire the path that was covered by trees. This path had helped her for many years.

But as she turned a slight left, staying on the path, things looked different. She was worried. She didn’t remember the trees looking like that. And the paved path suddenly became a dirt path. She was incredibly confused by this, because the path had always been paved and there was no way that she had gotten lost. She walked this path all the time. So, she turned around to escape the feeling but the trees seemed to close her in and she couldn’t make it back to the street. There was no way that she was lost.

But things were different and she didn’t know how to get out. She yelled and cried and sat on the dirt. She tried calling her parents but her phone wouldn’t work. There were houses nearby, but every time she tried to walk near them, they got further and further away. She was suffering, looking at the houses but not being able to go in. Stuck in a liminal space forever. She worried if anyone would find her. On this path that was no longer the same path. She worried what her parents and her girlfriend would think. Would time stand still? Or would they think that she ran away or worse? It ripped her heart out and she lay on the dirt and let time pass, unable to move at all.

The Bride in the Square

I love you, but we have never met. But you have told me that you love me many times in your letters to me. To us. It is anything but one-sided. I hope you know that. I read your words with sadness and longing and hope. To finally meet you one day.

I walk the streets that you once walked. I turn every corner hoping to see you there, but I know that you are in London. But I still hope.

I imagine you at 22. Singing in a band. I feel anachronistic, waiting for you. I would kiss you in the Yard. Or near the red line. I would fall in love with a bride, standing as still as possible. I would visit you on the street and give you money with my love notes.

This wine is for you. Please take it. Taste me like the wine.

We would get married in front of all of the people on Massachusetts Avenue. People wouldn’t even stop, but they would be our witnesses. Maybe you will wear your wedding dress.

We’ll buy wedding gifts for each other at the Raven. Maybe I would buy you a book on composers. Or philosophy or medieval poetry.

We’ll sit on a blanket in the Yard and share wedding dessert. Some people would stare, some people would walk right by. And that would be fine with us. You would sing songs to me, to everyone in the Square. Songs about our death and our wedding and sex and coffee. About revolution and sleep. Then we would take the red line back, in our wedding outfits, and fall in love with everyone on the subway.

We’d walk along the Charles and kiss in front of the skyline. And maybe no one would notice. The city will be our wedding and our song. The city would know.

Or perhaps, if you were back in Cambridge, you would walk right by me and not notice me. But I would know, and I would see my future slip away. The reality of future brides, now lost.

15,512,500

I think I have a hard time being around people who talk a lot because I understand words and I know that they are valuable and have power and you cannot waste them. You only get so many words in your life. Imagine learning that number. Imagine learning the amount of words that you will say in your lifetime. Well, here we are. They put computers into our brains that track every word that we say, and our tombstones will have the number of words spoken under the years.

Written words are different. They don’t add to your total, unless you read them out loud. That’s why writing has become so valuable. We don’t know the cap. The number of words that you can speak in your lifetime. They’ve never told us. Some people guess 200,000,00. Some guess lower, like maybe 50,000,000. Some think it’s as high as a billion. Each person has a different secret cap, so people lose their voices at different ages. It’s unique to the individual. But really, there’s no way to learn when your voice will get shut off.

There are always those stories of people using up their words and then becoming mute at a young age. There was that cult in Arizona, I’m sure you’ve heard about them on the news, who dedicates themselves to using up all of their words and then killed themselves when they use up every last word. And there are families that go generations without saying a word to one another. Losing your words out of fear of losing your words.

I heard a rumor on the train once that you can add words to your life. There’s some sort of computer program that can hack into your brain chip and make the number higher. MIT guys, from the looks of them. The guys said that it was extremely dangerous, because one wrong line of code could kill you. I know that a lot of people would probably risk it.

If you were shy, people sometimes thought that you were greedy. But we know the power of time and of words.

You could think that we’d never talk to each other because of that fear of losing your words. But even with the advent of technology, we still talk all of the time. And it’s not that I’m selfish or don’t want to be voiceless. I just understand the power of taking the time to say something, and the value of solitude.

Hell is empty and all the devils are here

“Why the hell do we have to go to this meeting?” Aamon said as he hopped down from his perch. His concrete skin moved as he stretched.
Sitting curled up all day will really destroy your back.
“Are you kidding? We invented staff meetings, remember? I think that was Dantalion’s doing. And yes, why the hell exactly, because Hell asked us to” Milton replied from his nearby perch.

They climbed up the side of the building and met in the bell tower. The hundreds of gargoyles and grotesques that decorated the outside of the building had a meeting every two weeks. Usually on Tuesdays at 2am, but sometimes there was a scheduling conflict and they had to move it to Wednesdays. Demons love scheduling conflicts.

`Now, imagine the worst staff meeting you’ve ever had to go to. The longest presentation delivered by a person who had a cold, but also has a very monotonous voice. And everyone is hungry and tired and time barely moves at all. It’s sort of like that, except much worse.

Once they reached the bell tower, Aamon and Milton flew around the rafters a bit and then perched on an empty beam. They did a lot of perching. For almost 900 years actually. Needless to say, even though they were demon spirits, their joints still ached. The rafters were full of gargoyles. All of them had concrete skin that could move; many of them had wings, abut only some of them had fangs. And they all worked for Satan himself, CEO of Hell.

“Hi everyone, thanks for coming today” Forneus said, moving his arm and attempting to quiet the group. Forneus always led the staff meetings because he did, after all, teach Rhetoric and languages. He held professional development meetings once a month on using language to temp and bribe and trick mortals. Other demons kept chatting, because being rude to a speaker is something that demons specialize in. Forneus continued, saying “Just some reminders before we get to our group activity and main presentation on demonic technologies!” Forneus waved his hands in what could only be described as jazz hands. Forneus really had too much excitement and energy for 2 in the morning. Usually at this hour, when they didn’t have staff meetings, Aamon would be either sleeping or terrorizing drunk Parisians, but he never did his job with this much excitement and gusto. All of the other grotesques basically hated Forneus, but he was too excited about meetings to notice.
“A reminder that, during the day, we are supposed to sit still on the side of the building as a way to prevent the prayers from the Cathedral of making any impact. Because of all of the tourists and their new age telephone cameras—which we’ll be learning more about today in our presentation! —they might notice if a bunch of us were missing. If you need to run a demonic errand and leave your post, just let one of your supervisors know. But for the most part, please stay on your perches and don’t interact too much with the humans.”
A hand shot up.
“Yes Halphas?” Forneus said, clearly excited that someone was taking an interest.
“But what if a tourist tried to fucking touch me, can I eat his head?”
Forneus sighed. This was something that got asked a lot. “Please try to limit the number of tourists that you eat or harm during the day. It really can cause quite a mess. At night you can mostly do whatever you want. It’s encouraged that demons terrorize humans at night and make their miserable lives more difficult” he said with a smile “but during the day it draws a lot of attention and we don’t need any more calls from down below. Remember, the tourists must think that we’re just part of the architecture.”
There was a lot of signing and eye-rolling from the crowd of grotesques.

“So remember!” Forneus said “our focus is to terrorize humans at night and prevent prayers during the day. I think we have lost our focus a bit as demons. We really want to be creating new problems for humans. Now, let’s transition to group work on brainstorming new ideas for pain and suffering.”
“This group work is causing me pain and suffering” Milton whispered to Aamon.
“Well that’s what we’ll put for number one then, ‘group work.’”

Aamon didn’t really like his job. Well, he liked the bit where he got to annoy and terrorize people. That was always quite amusing. But he hated all of the other bits—the meetings, reporting back to the boss, having to stay at the Cathedral. He supposed it was better than the jobs some of the other demons had. His friend Dante had a desk job editing memos and notetaking for Satan’s meetings. Aamon didn’t really put a lot of effort into his job—until, he got the best idea that he had in centuries.

When Forneus saw the spire burning, he thought, this is going to be a lot of paperwork. It was almost 7:00pm; it was a bit dark, and the grotesques were just getting ready for their evening torments. Servants' Staircase

The home was unlike any I had ever seen. I believed that it grew and changed. Some days, I would walk downstairs and see a door that I would have sworn wasn't there before. Everything seemed to be infinite. Hidden compartments, secret stairs, and floorboards that sounded as if they were talking to you as you walked on them. Maybe they were.

I loved the house the moment I saw it. It was many years ago now. In that moment I knew that my days would probably end there too. But it would be worth it.

My room was on the third floor. At the time, I was renting the room. It became mine. An old ornate rug covered a section of the hardwood floor. My bed was pushed into the corner. Because the wall was slanted, my bed always felt like it was being pulled into the wall, as if the room itself would eat me alive.

The door didn't close all the way. Even back then, I spent most of my time in my room. The people that I was renting from were nice enough. I did not know them before, but a friend of a friend's relative was looking to rent out some rooms. The house was Victorian. I lived on the third floor, where there were two other bedrooms besides my own and a small alcove in the hallway with two chairs.

Supposedly, there was a man who also lived on the third floor with me, or so I was told by Rose, the owner of the house. But I never saw him. I would listen, day in and day out, for footsteps to walk by my room and down the small servants’ staircase. When the house was first built, the servants had lived on the third floor and had used the side staircase that lead down to the kitchen. I was not a servant to Rose, but I served that house for my entire life.

Rose would often speak of him. Jefferson was his name. She would leave out tea for him, and it would go untouched.

One day I could not handle it any longer. I needed to know if Jefferson was real. I walked down the curved hallway and knocked on his door. At that point, I had been there for an entire year and I had never seen him. Never to get food, or use the bathroom. Many times, I stayed up all night to see if perhaps he was awake during the night, like an animal. But I heard nothing.

On that fateful day when I knocked on the door to Jefferson's room, I did not hear a response. Perhaps he was hard of hearing, I thought. My first knock, "rap-rap-rap, rap " was a bit soft due to my nervousness. But my second knock, " rap-rap-rap-rap,rap, rap-rap " was louder. Still, nothing. I tried the door, and it was locked. I gave up and figured that perhaps Rose made him up. Perhaps she was thinking of her long-dead brother, Joseph. The house had been in her family for quite a while, and she was very old.

But that night, I was awoken by the sound of the exact same knocks on my bedroom door. When I was too nervous to answer, the knocking started up again, with frantic insistency. I grabbed my knife and pulled open the door, desperately wanting to see Jefferson's face, the unknown face that had followed me everywhere in my mind. But when I opened the door, there was no one in the hallway.

Rose always made tea in the mornings. I would drink my cup or two while she would go on about her family. She had a daughter and a son, both in their fifties, who would visit. They seemed nice enough. Rose, worked part time teaching music lessons and working as a seamstress. I would often hear sad music crawl up to the third floor, the most haunting sounds I had ever heard. Often, her dressing mannequin would be found in the strangest of places. Once in the bathtub. Once in the basement. And once in my closet.

She once tried to make me a shirt. A button-down with a stiff collar; it was the most beautiful shade of emerald green with intricate gold buttons. She somehow knew my exact size, although she had never taken my measurements. I wore the shirt to my job one day, and when I came home, I could not seem to get the shirt off. I went to unbutton the shirt, and I could not get a single button. I tried for twenty minutes, panic filling my lungs. I wasn't frustrated, but afraid. I caught my reflection in the mirror, trying to take the shirt off. I looked completely unrecognizable. Wild and nervous and panicked. The buttons were normal, the shirt was normal, but I was different.

Finally, I ripped the buttons off the shirt and desperately pulled the fabric off. My skin was stained dark green and I was sweating. My skin also appeared to be covered in scratch marks, though from what I had no idea. I hung the shirt in the back on my closet and was afraid to even look at it again for three years.

It seemed that things changed in the house every day. Lamps moved, plates seemed to disappear and reappear. At first, I thought perhaps I just wasn't very observant, but I noticed everything. As much as the house haunted me, I loved every inch of it. The paint that was chipping and fading. The cold nights and hot summers. I couldn't move out because the thought of renting a cold, small apartment somewhere made me fear for my life. I was lonely in the house, but I knew myself too well. I knew that if I lived anywhere else, I would rot away.

I met folks at school. Women who were kind to me and would buy me coffees. They would ask me about my day, and for a few weeks or a month I'd see them. But I still somehow felt more alone. I never brought them to the house. They would invite me to their apartments for the night or for a few days, but the rooms would quickly start to feel like they belonged to Giovanni, and I would leave and go back to the house. The house was waiting.

I finished school and got a proper full-time job. I would awake early to catch my train, and somehow tea was always waiting for me. No matter what time I awoke, there would always be tea waiting. I would often wake up at 2am and pad down to the kitchen. The tea would be warm. I was a light sleeper, always awake in the middle of the night. I would have heard the kettle from my room if someone had made the tea, despite being three floors up. The house was like an echo chamber, and I could hear everything. And yet I never heard the kettle. When I asked Rose if she had made the tea, she answered " no dear, I was asleep. " I tested out my curiosity. I awoke at all hours of the morning over multiple weeks. And every time there was always hot tea waiting.

I had been in the house for seven years years. Friends and family would ask when I was moving out. I said that I didn't know. I could afford to move out. It wasn't Rose's company that I desired either. Sometimes I wouldn't see her for weeks at a time, but she would always come back. She always had some story—seeing friends, or staying with family. She would always come back, until she didn't.

When I hadn’t seen Rose in a month, I called her. I thought perhaps she had gone to visit her sister and hadn’t told me, which happened often. But there was no answer. I called her sister too, and I never heard back either. I reported Rose as missing to the police, but they said that, while they would check up on it, perhaps she had just moved and there was really nothing they could do.

I didn’t believe them. When I called to see if they had found Rose, they assured me not to worry. They hadn’t found her, but she was probably fine. They said that, unless there was any evidence in the house of her going missing, they couldn’t really do anything. I was angry at their dismissiveness.

But I stayed at the house. Rose had always told me to take good care of the house, and so I did just that. After that, I was alone in the house. I thought about renting it out, but I suppose I never got around to it. I thought about Molly, and how she got sick. And part of me believed that the house had made her sick.

During those first few years, Rose had a few lodgers rent out the other rooms in the house. But I was the only one who stayed. Everyone always had a reason to leave, and I never did.

Molly was smart and beautiful and in school at the same time that I was. She was studying to be a social worker. Molly and I would talk over tea in the mornings, which would turn into talking late into the night in my room. For a while, she would stay in my room more nights than not. But eventually, Molly got sick. She had a hard time walking. I would read to her and bring her food. I would spend hours reading her favorite books to her. She smiled at me and she might have even loved me.

But she left school and went home for her parents to take care of her. She wrote me a few letters, which I still have. In the letters, she never said what her illness was, and I didn’t want to ask. Perhaps I feared that she was terminally ill. But after a month or so I never heard from her again. Once I tried to visit her—I went to the address on the return label. But the house was empty and barely standing.

Whenever I had people over for dinner, they would always remark on how beautiful the house was. They would jokingly ask if they could move in. And I thought about it, once or twice. These were my friends. I came home to this empty house every night. But I could never do it, and I didn’t know why.

I was 32; it had been 10 years. Rose never came back, and I took care of the house. I scrubbed the kitchen and swept the creaky floorboards. I hung up the old curtains and put out old photos of family that did not belong to me. I always felt like I was preparing the house for someone, or something, but I never knew what.

At that point, Audrey began to live with me. I cared about her a lot. I had a lot of space, and I liked being around her. But she seemed to change, after. She would throw things when I wasn’t in the room. She said people were following her, and she was trying to scare them away.

She burned some of my books. She said paper and ink were not worth a person’s life. I never understood what she meant by that, and when I tried to ask once, she would just cry.

Audrey would say that she felt like she was suffocating in the house. I thought perhaps that she meant that I was spending too much time with her, or that she had changed her mind about our relationship. But she said she felt like the house itself was pressing down on her. There were a lot of rooms, but she said that even in such a large house she still felt like she was in a coffin. She sometimes she would wake up in the middle of the night gasping for air or screaming. I would hold her until she calmed down.

Eventually, she left. She was sick as well, her lungs failing her. She couldn’t climb up the staircases. I tried suggesting that we could move out. But she said that she had to go to the country and spend time recovering.

As much as the house weighed down on my mind, I couldn’t leave. I still had to take care of it. I was never cold, and I never felt lonely either. The hidden doors and notes in the closets and chipping paint were a reminder, but a reminder of what I never figured out. It was like a history on the tip of my tongue.

I know that my days will end here. Amongst the old paintings and trunks of clothes and cracked vases. I don’t know who will find me, or if I will be found at all. I’ll burn all of my journals. The house will perhaps claim me as part of it, and will keep on going without me.
